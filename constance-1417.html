Constance — the Six Locks

Thirty-nine years of Schism. Three claimants deposed or resigned; the last, old Pedro de Luna, still calls himself Benedict XIII on his rock at Peñíscola. On 8 November 1417 fifty-three electors climb the stairs of the merchant hall above the Bodensee: twenty-three cardinals of three broken obediences and thirty deputies of the five nations. A pope needs two-thirds of the Sacred College and two-thirds of every nation — sixteen cardinals and four votes from each of five benches. Six locks. One throne. Christendom is waiting outside in the snow.

Not in the Kaufhaus: Baldassare Cossa, the deposed John XXIII, sits in a Palatine prison; Pedro de Luna keeps his tiny court at Peñíscola, and his last loyal cardinals — Murillo, Urriés, Carrillo de Albornoz and Fonseca — with him. Louis de Bar, Morosini, Carbone and Isolani (John's legate holding Rome) are absent; Repington of Lincoln never took his hat. The autumn buried the rest: Hallum of Salisbury on 4 September, Cardinal Zabarella on the 26th, old Gregory XII himself at Recanati on 18 October, and Cardinal Stefaneschi on the month's last day.
