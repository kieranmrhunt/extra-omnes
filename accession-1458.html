The Accession

Rome · 16–19 August 1458 · eighteen cardinals · twelve elect

Calixtus III is dead and his Catalans are hunted through the Banchi. Capranica, the man this conclave was meant to crown, died four days before it opened, of the fever that has not left the city. Eighteen men are walled into two chapels in the Roman August; the French party has a fortune, a candidate and, by Friday night, a written pact sworn in the latrines — eleven voices, one short of a pope. What stands against it is a poor, gouty poet from Siena, a counter-meeting at dawn in the Dean's cell, and the newest, strangest instrument in the Church's electoral law: the accessio, by which a man may rise after the count and carry his voice, out loud, with everyone watching, to another's total.

«Et ego Senensi Cardinali accedo, eumque Papam facio.»And I accede to the Cardinal of Siena — and make him Pope. · Prospero Colonna, 19 August 1458, shaking two cardinals off his arms
